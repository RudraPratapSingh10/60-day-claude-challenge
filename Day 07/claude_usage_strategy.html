Why This Model Fits Me

You're a student coding daily, which means most of your requests are debugging, writing functions, understanding concepts, or quick checks — not research papers or month-long strategy documents. Sonnet 4.6 is built exactly for this: it's fast enough for daily back-and-forth, strong at code, and far cheaper than Opus on a student budget. You don't need Opus-level depth for 80% of what you described — you need a model you can keep open all day without it feeling slow or expensive.

Model Tiers
When to Use Haiku

Reach for Haiku 4.5 when you just need a quick, low-stakes answer — syntax lookups, "what does this error mean," rewriting a sentence, a one-line explanation, or any task where speed matters more than depth. Think of it as your "Slack reply," not your "design doc."

When to Use Sonnet

Sonnet 4.6 should be your default for nearly everything: writing and debugging code, explaining concepts while you study, drafting assignments, working through homework logic, and most daily Q&A. It's the model you should leave selected unless you have a specific reason to switch.

When to Use Opus

Switch to Opus (4.6, 4.7, or 4.8) when a task is genuinely hard or high-stakes: a multi-file refactor, a tricky algorithm or architecture decision, a research paper requiring synthesis across many sources, a business/strategy plan with real tradeoffs, or any project where being wrong is costly (a final submission, a thesis chapter, a real client deliverable). Don't use it for routine homework — that's overkill.

Recommended Effort Level

Anthropic's effort dial controls how much internal reasoning a model does before answering — it trades thoroughness against speed and cost, and works the same way whether you're in claude.ai or the API. By default, Claude uses high effort, spending as many tokens as needed for excellent results.

⚠ Biggest Mistakes I Should Avoid

Running everything on Opus "just to be safe" wastes both money and time — most of your daily coding and studying doesn't need that depth. The opposite mistake is just as common: defaulting to Max effort on simple tasks. It's not a quality guarantee — Max effort makes Claude try harder, not be right; on a question it would get wrong at High, it can still get wrong at Max, just more expensively and slower. Save Opus and Max for the moments that actually demand them — a hard bug, a real research task, a high-stakes deliverable — and let Sonnet at standard effort carry your daily load.
