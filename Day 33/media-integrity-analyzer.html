Step 1 · Orientation

Every day you're the last editor a story has.

Headlines are written to earn a click. Posts are written to earn a reaction. Neither is written, first and foremost, to inform you. This isn't about cynicism — it's about noticing the craft. You'll practice on two short exercises, then see how your instincts scored.

Step 2 · The idea

A headline's job is attention. A fact's job is accuracy. They aren't always the same job.

Newsrooms and creators alike know that certain words — secretly, banned, destroyed, furious, quietly — earn more clicks than neutral ones. That doesn't automatically make a headline false. But it means the headline can drift from what the article actually supports, especially around words that imply intent (secretly), scale (thousands), or danger (destroying).

In everyday life: this is why a story about a routine schedule change can be framed as outrage, or a standard tax credit can be framed as a scandal. You'll read one below, then decide for yourself before we show you the gap.

Step 3 · The idea

Strong feelings make weak fact-checkers.

Fear, outrage, and urgency all narrow your attention — that's their biological job. Content designed to provoke those feelings quickly tends to get shared before it gets verified. Recognizing the technique (urgency, scarcity, moral outrage, fear) is often easier than judging the claim itself, and it's a faster warning sign.

In everyday life: this is the difference between "a company changed its policy" and "you won't believe what they're hiding." Same event, very different emotional design.
